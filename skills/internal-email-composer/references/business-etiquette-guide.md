# Business Email Etiquette Guide

This reference provides cultural and professional guidelines for composing internal business emails in Japanese and English contexts.

## Japanese Business Email Etiquette (日本語ビジネスメールマナー)

### 1. Subject Line (件名)

**Best Practices:**
- Include email type prefix: 【依頼】【報告】【確認】【連絡】【相談】【お礼】
- Keep concise but descriptive (15-30 characters)
- Include project name or key identifier when relevant

**Examples:**
- 【依頼】見積書作成のお願い
- 【報告】週次進捗報告（12月第2週）
- 【確認】会議日程について
- 【再送】先日のご依頼について

### 2. Greetings (挨拶)

**Standard Opening Patterns:**
- 「お疲れ様です」- Most common internal greeting
- 「お世話になっております」- For external or formal internal
- 「いつもお世話になっております」- Slightly more formal

**Seasonal Greetings (時候の挨拶):**
Generally omitted in internal emails for brevity, but may include:
- 年始: 本年もよろしくお願いいたします
- 年末: 年末のお忙しい中恐れ入りますが

### 3. Self-Introduction (自己紹介)

**Format:**
```
{部署名}の{氏名}です。
```

**Examples:**
- システム開発部の山田です。
- 営業企画課の佐藤と申します。

### 4. Body Structure (本文構成)

**Follow 起承転結 Pattern:**

1. **起 (Introduction)**: State purpose clearly in first sentence
2. **承 (Development)**: Provide context and details
3. **転 (Turn)**: Address potential concerns or alternatives
4. **結 (Conclusion)**: Clear call to action and closing

**Formatting Guidelines:**
- Use bullet points (・) for lists
- Use 【】for section headers
- Keep paragraphs short (2-3 sentences)
- Include line breaks for readability

### 5. Request Expressions (依頼表現)

**Politeness Levels (from most to least polite):**

| Level | Expression | Usage |
|-------|------------|-------|
| Very Polite | ～いただけますでしょうか | To superiors, important requests |
| Polite | ～いただけますか | Standard internal requests |
| Moderate | ～お願いできますか | Peer-to-peer requests |
| Direct | ～お願いします | Routine tasks |

### 6. Apology/Consideration Phrases (クッション言葉)

**Before Requests:**
- お忙しいところ恐れ入りますが
- お手数をおかけしますが
- ご多用中恐れ入りますが
- 突然のお願いで恐縮ですが

**Acknowledging Inconvenience:**
- ご面倒をおかけしますが
- お手間を取らせてしまい恐縮ですが

### 7. Closing Phrases (結び)

**Standard Closings:**
- よろしくお願いいたします。(Most common)
- よろしくお願い申し上げます。(More formal)
- ご確認のほど、よろしくお願いいたします。(Confirmation requests)
- ご検討いただければ幸いです。(Proposals)
- お返事お待ちしております。(Awaiting reply)

### 8. Signature Block (署名)

**Standard Format:**
```
---
{氏名}
{部署名}
{連絡先情報}
```

## English Business Email Etiquette

### 1. Subject Line

**Best Practices:**
- Action-oriented when appropriate: "Action Required:", "FYI:", "Request:"
- Specific and searchable
- 5-10 words maximum

**Examples:**
- Request: Q4 Budget Review by Dec 15
- FYI: Updated Project Timeline
- Action Required: Vendor Selection Decision

### 2. Salutation

**Formality Levels:**

| Context | Salutation | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Formal | Dear + Title + Last Name | Dear Ms. Johnson, |
| Semi-formal | Dear + First Name | Dear Sarah, |
| Informal | Hello/Hi + First Name | Hi Sarah, |
| Group | Team/All | Team, / Hi all, |

### 3. Opening Lines

**Purpose-Driven Openers:**
- "I am writing to request..." (Requests)
- "I wanted to follow up on..." (Follow-ups)
- "Please find below..." (Updates/Reports)
- "I hope this email finds you well." (General, semi-formal)

**Avoid:**
- Overly casual openings in formal contexts
- Empty pleasantries that delay the point

### 4. Body Structure

**BLUF Principle (Bottom Line Up Front):**
1. State main point/request in first paragraph
2. Provide supporting details
3. Specify action items clearly
4. Include deadlines with dates

**Formatting:**
- Use bullet points for lists
- Bold key information (**deadline**, **action required**)
- Keep paragraphs to 3-4 sentences
- Use white space for readability

### 5. Tone Guidelines

**Professional but Warm:**
- Avoid overly stiff language
- Use contractions appropriately (I'm, we've)
- Be direct but polite
- Show appreciation genuinely

**Phrases to Use:**
- "I would appreciate if you could..."
- "Please let me know if you have any questions."
- "Thank you for your help with this."
- "Could you please..."

**Phrases to Avoid:**
- "ASAP" (specify actual deadline instead)
- "Per my last email" (sounds passive-aggressive)
- "Please advise" (vague)

### 6. Call to Action

**Be Specific:**
- ❌ "Please let me know your thoughts."
- ✅ "Please confirm the meeting time by Friday, Dec 15."

**Include:**
- Specific action required
- Clear deadline with date
- Contact for questions

### 7. Closing

**Formality Levels:**

| Context | Closing |
|---------|---------|
| Formal | Best regards, / Sincerely, |
| Semi-formal | Kind regards, / Thank you, |
| Informal | Thanks, / Best, |
| Appreciative | Many thanks, / Thank you for your help, |

### 8. Signature Block

**Standard Format:**
```
{Full Name}
{Title}
{Department}
{Phone/Email}
```

## Cross-Cultural Considerations

### Japanese → English Adaptation

When adapting Japanese content for English audiences:

1. **Reduce Honorifics**: Remove excessive politeness markers
2. **Be More Direct**: State requests clearly upfront
3. **Simplify Structure**: Less formal hierarchy in presentation
4. **Shorten Closings**: Single closing phrase is sufficient

### English → Japanese Adaptation

When adapting English content for Japanese audiences:

1. **Add Cushion Phrases**: Include consideration expressions
2. **Formal Register**: Use appropriate keigo level
3. **Context First**: May need more background before request
4. **Extended Closings**: Include proper closing formulas

## Urgency Level Guidelines

### Normal (通常)
- Standard response time expected (2-3 business days)
- No special urgency markers needed

### High (高)
- Response needed within 1 business day
- Japanese: 件名に「至急」を追加
- English: Add "Time-Sensitive" to subject

### Urgent (緊急)
- Same-day response required
- Japanese: 【緊急】prefix, consider phone follow-up
- English: "URGENT:" prefix, mention timeline explicitly

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Japanese
1. Missing self-introduction
2. Too direct without cushion phrases
3. Unclear call to action
4. Wrong honorific level for recipient
5. Missing closing phrase

### English
1. Buried lede (key point not upfront)
2. Vague deadlines ("soon", "ASAP")
3. Overly formal or stiff language
4. Missing specific action items
5. Too lengthy (respect reader's time)
