# Purchase Request Best Practices Guide

## Overview

This guide provides best practices for creating effective purchase request documents that maximize approval success while ensuring proper documentation and compliance.

## Purchase Request Components

### 1. Executive Summary

The executive summary should be concise (3-5 sentences) and include:
- What is being requested
- Total cost
- Key benefit or business need
- Recommended action

**Example:**
> This purchase request seeks approval for 10 Dell Latitude 5540 laptops ($12,500 total) to replace aging equipment for the Engineering team. Current laptops are 5+ years old, causing productivity loss estimated at $2,000/month. Approval is recommended to restore productivity and reduce support costs.

### 2. Product Specification

Include sufficient detail for approvers to understand what is being purchased:

| Element | Required | Details |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Product Name | Yes | Full product name and model |
| Vendor | Yes | Primary vendor or source |
| Specifications | Yes | Key technical specifications |
| Quantity | Yes | Number of units |
| Unit Price | Yes | Price per unit |
| Total Cost | Yes | Total including taxes, shipping |
| Warranty | Recommended | Warranty terms and coverage |
| Delivery | Recommended | Expected delivery timeline |

### 3. Business Justification

Strong justifications should address these questions:

1. **What problem does this solve?**
   - Current state and pain points
   - Impact of the problem on operations

2. **Why is this solution the right choice?**
   - Alignment with business objectives
   - Why this product over alternatives

3. **What are the consequences of not purchasing?**
   - Risks of delay or inaction
   - Opportunity costs

### 4. Cost-Benefit Analysis

#### Cost Categories

**Direct Costs:**
- Purchase price
- Installation/setup
- Training
- Licensing fees

**Indirect Costs:**
- Maintenance
- Support time
- Consumables
- Power/infrastructure

**Total Cost of Ownership (TCO):**
Calculate over the expected useful life (typically 3-5 years):

```
TCO = Initial Cost + (Annual Operating Cost × Years) + Disposal Cost
```

#### Benefit Categories

**Quantifiable Benefits:**
- Time savings (hours × hourly rate)
- Cost reduction (specific amount)
- Revenue increase (projected)
- Error reduction (cost of errors × reduction rate)

**Non-Quantifiable Benefits:**
- Employee satisfaction
- Competitive advantage
- Risk mitigation
- Compliance improvement

### 5. Financial Metrics

#### Return on Investment (ROI)

```
ROI = ((Total Benefits - Total Costs) / Total Costs) × 100
```

**Interpretation:**
- ROI > 100%: Investment returns more than it costs
- ROI = 100%: Break-even
- ROI < 100%: Investment costs more than it returns

#### Payback Period

```
Payback Period (months) = Total Initial Cost / Monthly Benefit
```

**Guidelines:**
- < 12 months: Strong payback
- 12-24 months: Acceptable payback
- 24-36 months: Extended payback (needs justification)
- > 36 months: Weak payback (consider alternatives)

#### Net Present Value (NPV)

```
NPV = Σ (Cash Flow_t / (1 + r)^t) - Initial Investment
```

Where:
- r = discount rate (typically 8-12%)
- t = time period (years)

**Interpretation:**
- NPV > 0: Investment creates value
- NPV = 0: Break-even
- NPV < 0: Investment destroys value

## Vendor Comparison

### Evaluation Criteria

When comparing vendors, consider:

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Price | 25-35% | Total cost including all fees |
| Quality | 20-25% | Product quality and reliability |
| Support | 15-20% | Technical support and responsiveness |
| Warranty | 10-15% | Coverage terms and duration |
| Delivery | 10-15% | Lead time and reliability |
| Reputation | 5-10% | Market standing and reviews |

### Scoring Method

Use a 1-5 scale for each criterion:
- 5 = Excellent
- 4 = Good
- 3 = Acceptable
- 2 = Below Average
- 1 = Poor

**Weighted Score Calculation:**
```
Total Score = Σ (Criterion Score × Weight)
```

### Minimum Vendor Requirements

- Vendor must be in good standing (no active litigation, bankruptcy)
- Vendor must provide proof of insurance
- Vendor must accept standard payment terms
- Vendor must provide written warranty

## Approval Thresholds

Typical approval authority levels (adjust for your organization):

| Amount | Approver |
|--------|----------|
| < $1,000 | Manager |
| $1,000 - $5,000 | Director |
| $5,000 - $25,000 | VP |
| $25,000 - $100,000 | CFO |
| > $100,000 | CEO/Board |

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Vague justification**: "We need this" without explaining why
2. **Missing alternatives**: Not documenting other options considered
3. **Incomplete costs**: Forgetting training, installation, ongoing costs
4. **Unrealistic benefits**: Overstating expected returns
5. **Missing urgency**: Not explaining why timing matters
6. **Incomplete specifications**: Insufficient detail for evaluation

## Document Checklist

Before submitting a purchase request, verify:

- [ ] Executive summary is clear and complete
- [ ] Product specifications are detailed
- [ ] Vendor is identified with contact info
- [ ] Pricing is current (quotes attached)
- [ ] Business justification addresses the "why"
- [ ] Cost-benefit analysis is complete
- [ ] ROI/payback calculations are accurate
- [ ] Alternatives were considered and documented
- [ ] Budget availability confirmed
- [ ] Correct approval routing identified
- [ ] Supporting documents attached (quotes, specs)

## Presentation Tips for Approval Meetings

1. **Lead with the ask**: State what you need approval for immediately
2. **Focus on business impact**: Translate technical benefits to business outcomes
3. **Prepare for questions**: Anticipate concerns about cost, timing, alternatives
4. **Have backup data**: Be ready to provide additional detail if requested
5. **Know the budget**: Understand where funds will come from
6. **Present options**: Show alternatives with pros/cons
7. **End with recommendation**: Clearly state what action you're requesting
